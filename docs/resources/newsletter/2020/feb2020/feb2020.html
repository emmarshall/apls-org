PRESIDENTIAL COLUMN

We are fast approaching our time together in New Orleans at the American Psychology-Law Society Annual Conference! I hope you are as excited as I am to see some great presentations, get involved in the Division, and catch up with friends and colleagues. As I’m sure you saw in your emails, the final program grid has been released. It can be found here. There are many exciting sessions offered! First, check out this year’s slate of pre-conference CE offerings. In keeping with my presidential initiative about expanding our research to address the needs of judges, the Presidential Plenary this year will be an “in conversation” with judges about their practice and research interests. There will be other fabulous special sessions offered by our hard-working committees that I want to highlight here. On Thursday, the Student Section will be offering a session titled “Bridging the Gap: Confinement in the Juvenile Justice System.” On Friday, the Research Committee will be offering a session discussing open science titled “Opening the Door for Better Science in Psychology and Law: The Benefits of Open Science Practices,” the Minority Affairs Committee will be offering “Preventing Wrongful Convictions & Bringing Innocent Clients Home,” and the Early Career Professionals Committee will present “Identifying & Meeting the Needs of Early Career Professionals in Law & Psychology.” On Saturday, the Teaching, Training, and Careers Committee will host “Influencing Change: The Varied Lives of Policy Work,” and the Professional Development of Women Committee will present “Terrified & Doing It Anyway: Embracing Your Inner Imposter.” Make sure you schedule time to check out some of the amazing work our committees are doing! All are invited to attend the Business Meeting on Thursday evening from 6-6:30, where you can learn about news in the Division and opportunities to get involved. Finally, don’t miss your chance to support the Student Section by participating in the 5k Fun Run!


LEGAL UPDATE: RESOLUTION OR RESIGNATION: THE ROLE OF FORENSIC MENTAL HEALTH PROFESSIONALS AMIDST THE COMPETENCY SERVICES CRISIS

Competency-related cases often occur in under-resourced, over-taxed systems of evaluation and care, despite the efforts of dedicated personnel found working in those systems. Forensic psychologists may have difficulty adhering to overarching ethical principles if our work occurs unabated in the midst of the inhumane conditions that often surround competency-related services. How should forensic professionals seek to resolve this challenge, if at all?

NORMAN J. FINKEL

I regret to inform the Division that one of our distinguished members, Norman J. Finkel passed away last month on December 2, 2019. Norm Finkel spent most of his career in the Psychology Department at Georgetown University. He was on the faculty for thirty-seven years, beginning in 1970. He received his bachelor’s degree in Psychology from N.Y.U., where he studied with Phil Zimbardo, and his Ph.D. in Clinical Psychology from Rochester University. At Georgetown, he taught Abnormal Psychology and Psychology and Law, as well as advanced seminars in Theories of Therapy, and Madness, Genius, and Creativity. In 2005, he received the College Dean’s Award for Excellence in Undergraduate Teaching, reflecting his deep devotion to undergraduate students whom he taught with great dedication, flair, and with a particular focus on deepening students’ thinking and critical capacities. He was a leader in the field of psychology and law. During his career, he wrote many influential books in the field including Therapy and Ethics: The Courtship of Law and Psychology (1980), Mental Illness and Health (1982), Insanity on Trial (1988), Commonsense Justice: Jurors’ Notions of the Law (1995), Not Fair! The Typology of Commonsense Unfairness (2001), The Psychology of Rights and Duties: Empirical Contributions and Normative Commentaries (2005), and Emotions and Culpability: How the Law Is at Odds with Psychology, Jurors, and Itself (2006). He is survived by his daughter Jennifer, his sister Debbie, and countless friends and family, including his good friend Steve Sabat, Emeritus at Georgetown, who provided me with much of the information above from his own tribute to Norm for the department.

This is a great personal loss for me, as well as a loss for our field. I first met Norm as a student in his Theories of Therapy class at Georgetown. He was an exceptional teacher – funny, quirky, and engaging. There always was an element of theater in his teaching. If I’m being honest, I was an unexceptional student in that class. That was not our moment. In the fall of my senior year, I was still at a bit of a loss for what area of psychology interested me most. I knew I wanted to pursue something in the area of criminal psychology, but I wasn’t sure what or how. That’s when I took his Psychology and Law class, and it changed my life. Not only did it inspire me intellectually to pursue work in this field, but it also gave me a roadmap for how to teach more generally. In that class, there were so many “Jennifer”s (as is the curse of many people my age), that he gave us all nicknames. Mine was “44” because my middle name is Leigh, and Lee Grosscup (no relation to my knowledge) was an NFL Football player who I guess played with that number. At the very bottom of his syllabus for the class he encouraged anyone interested in doing research to contact him. I had never seen that before as a student. At the time, I had no idea what that really meant but I thought why not? I didn’t know going in what a prolific researcher he was or how important that experience would be for me. I worked with him my whole senior year, learning how to design studies, collect data, and most importantly, how to “think big” about the impact of psychology on the legal system.

I continued to work with him for three years after graduation until I went to graduate and law school. He was a constant guiding force for me, providing me with opportunities, advice, and new knowledge. I know I would not be where I am today, in this field and in a career that I love, without his mentoring. Now, as a professor, I try to emulate his enthusiasm for learning and his openness and dedication to undergraduate research and education. I put a similar blurb at the bottom of the syllabus in every class I teach, inviting students to contact me if they are interested in research. I tell them this story. I have met and mentored some of the most amazing people because of this – because of him. I’m sure many of you have a “Norm” in your lives that sparked your interest in psychology and law. Take a minute to let them know, and never underestimate your own ability to light that spark for someone else.

In fond remembrance,

Jen Groscup (44)

If you would like to make a donation to honor his memory, the family has suggested the ACLU and the Holocaust Memorial in Washington DC as organizations with missions close to his heart.

Associate Editor

llevett@ufl.edu

Announcing the 2020 Law and Human Behavior Reviewer Mentoring Program!

Have you always wondered how people get to be reviewers for prestigious journals? Are you interested in becoming a reviewer for LHB (the official journal of AP-LS), but don’t know what that means or how to get training and experience with providing strong reviews? Do you have limited or no access to a mentor that can provide you with this type of experience? If so, now’s your chance to develop your reviewer skills!

The LHB editorial team is continuing their Reviewer Mentoring Program in 2020 to provide current graduate students and ECPs (within 10 years of last terminal degree) with training and mentoring specifically in conducting strong peer reviews. If you will be attending the AP-LS conference in March 2020 and do not have access to this type of mentoring in your current graduate program, we invite you to apply for the program.

We will be selecting a small pilot group of Reviewers to participate in a training workshop at the 2020 AP-LS conference and then matching those participants with Reviewer Mentors. Mentees will then be assigned several manuscripts to review throughout 2020, under the supervision and guidance of their Reviewer Mentor.

If you would like to be considered for this program, please complete the application at the following link by February 5 (https://www.surveymonkey.com/r/LHBReviewing).
