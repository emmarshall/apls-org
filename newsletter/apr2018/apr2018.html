APA UPDATE

The same weekend as the APLS conference in Memphis, the APA Council of Representatives ("Council") met in Washington, D.C. APLS was represented by Jason Cantone and Joel Lieberman. 


At this meeting, Council voted to move forward on accrediting master's level psychology programs and approved guidelines on core learning goals for master's degree graduates in psychology.This occurred after about 70 years of discussion on the topic of master's programs. 

APA also shared that, for the first time, there were more internship positions available than applicants. For the 432 unmatched students in the 2018 APPIC Phase I match, 457 APA/CPA-accredited internships were still open.

Council also voted to approve a major organizational structure change, with APA changing from a 501(c)(3) to a joint 501(c)(6) organization. Why does this matter? The changed tax status allows for more advocacy and fewer lobbying restrictions. APA promised that increased lobbying will be on behalf of science as much as for practitioner issues. All APA members will become members of this new joint organization when they renew next year. APA promised that dues will not go up for at least three years, but there is concern they will go up after that, because APAPO (APA's current 501(c)(6) to focus on practice issues) will no longer receive its own, separate dues. Final plans will be discussed at APA in August. 

In addition, APA is currently hiring a Chief Ethics Officer and will begin the process of hiring a Chief Diversity Officer later this year.

If you have any questions about these decisions or would like to know more about the other decisions at Council, including the passage of new clinical practice guidelines regarding obesity, please contact Jason Cantone (jasoncantone@gmail.com).

EXPERT OPINION: HOT TUBBING AS AN ALTERNATIVE TO ADVERSARIAL EXPERT TESTIMONY

Initially developed in Australian courts, concurrent expert testimony, or “hot tubbing,” is a practice in which two experts sit together in a witness box and provide concurrent testimony. Could this legal innovation decrease expert bias and increase juror comprehension in the United States legal system?

AP-LS AWARD FOR BEST UNDERGRAD PAPER

Recognizes an outstanding undergraduate research paper focused on the interdisciplinary study of psychology and law.


Deadline: June 30, 2018


To be eligible for an award, the student must be the major contributor to a project on a topic relevant to psychology and law (i.e., the student had primary responsibility for initiating and conducting the project even though the project will usually be conducted under the supervision of a mentor). Data collection should be complete. Winners will be encouraged to submit their work for presentation at the AP-LS Conference (as first authors). Students may submit their work during their first post-undergraduate year as long as the work was conducted during their undergraduate career.

2017 DISSERTATION AWARD WINNERS

Dr. Nathalie Brackmann is the 1st place dissertation award winner. Her dissertation, entitled “Are children really the poorer eyewitnesses: An analysis of counterintuitive developmental trends in eyewitness memory,” comprised a series of studies investigating the malleability of developmental trends in eyewitness memory and testing methods to improve witness recall. The reviewers were extremely impressed with the dissertation work. “The author strings together, elegantly, a series of multi-study manuscripts to review developmental trends in eyewitness memory.The author puts the work together thoughtfully and meaningfully. The work throughout is of high quality with rigorous methodology.” The originality of the research also came through: “From the formatting and organizational structure, to the examination of adolescents, there is a sense of novelty throughout.The author is pushing the field forward to investigate an old problem in a new vein.” In addition, the reviewers noted the substantial contribution of the research to both researcher and practitioner communities.  Dr. Brackmann completed her dissertation under the supervision of Dr. Merckelbach at Maastricht University and Dr. Granhag at the University of Gothenburg.


Dr. Melissa Colloff is the 2nd place dissertation award winner. Her dissertation, entitled “Eyewitness  identification performanceon lineups for distinctive suspects,” investigated how the three lineup techniques for distinctive suspects influence eyewitness identification performance. She found that all three methods enable witnesses to confidently make accurate decisions, regardless of age. The research addresses an important and under-researched area through a progression of five studies. The reviewers noted that the research provides “a compelling case in favor of police employing any one of the three examined methods to maintain lineup fairness in cases involving a suspect with a distinct facial feature—an important applied contribution.” Dr. Colloff completed her dissertation under the supervision of Dr. Wade at Warwick University.

 

Dr. Lauren Kois is the 3rd place dissertation award winner. Her dissertation, entitled “Criminal responsibility: A meta-analysis and study space,” systematically assessed predictors of criminal responsibility outcomes and identified significant study-level characteristics. Hers is the first meta-analysis in the area. The reviewers noted the dissertation was exceptionally strong in terms of presentation; the literature review was clear, the description of the meta-analysis process was meticulous, the findings were clear, and the discussion was thorough. They also noted the “strong contribution to identifying necessary lines of future research.” Dr. Kois completed her dissertation under the supervision of Dr. Chauhan at John Jay College, City University of New York.
