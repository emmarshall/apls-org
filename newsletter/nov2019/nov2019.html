KEEP AP-LS’ VOICE HEARD AT APA 

If you are a member of the American Psychological Association (APA), you should have received your 2019 APA Ballot Election via e-mail on November 1. It is important that you promptly respond to this email to help keep AP-LS's voice heard within APA.

Your vote will determine how many voting members AP-LS gets on APA's governing body, the Council of Representatives. Right now, AP-LS has two representatives. Depending on the number of votes that AP-LS gets during this ballot process, AP-LS could gain or lose a representative. Each APA member is given 10 votes. Please give all 10 of your votes to Division 41/AP-LS. 

This ballot also includes a proposed change to the APA bylaws to allow some graduate student members the right to vote in some APA elections, as further explained on the ballot. In August, the APA Council of Representatives (including the two AP-LS representatives) voted in favor (73.4%) of the item, and the APA Board of Directors recommended approval. 

If you have any questions, please contact Jason Cantone, at jasoncantone@gmail.com. If you are an APA member and did not receive the ballot email, please contact Garnett Coad, at gcoad@apa.org. 


UNDERSTANDING THE NEEDS OF AP-LS EARLY CAREER PROFESSIONALS

In late 2018, 119 AP-LS early career professionals (ECPs) completed a detailed survey regarding their graduate training, professional development, current career path, and recommendations for how AP-LS can meet the needs of ECPs. This survey provided a wealth of information. Some findings include: 

-  Diversity. Respondents overwhelmingly self-identified as White (81%) and female (83%) and noted a need for more diversity awareness and recruitment, as well as a desire for more training in socio-cultural issues in law and psychology, and harassment and discrimination claims. 

- Debt. Respondents’ average student debt load at the time of graduation was a little over $100,000, with about $89,000 attributable solely to graduate training. Respondents who received a PsyD reported the highest debt load. 

- Meeting ECP Needs.  Only 5% of respondents said that AP-LS addressed their needs extremely well, with the most saying moderately well. Respondents recommended that AP-LS provide further opportunities regarding peer networking, professional networking, and how to establish collaborations outside of academia. 

- Policy and Applied Areas. Respondents reported satisfaction with many aspects of their graduate training, but many expressed disappointment with a lack of guidance on going into the non-academic job market, and a need for more training in policy areas and better experiential learning opportunities. This was especially likely for social science respondents. 

The full report is available here. Questions can be directed to Jason Cantone, at jasoncantone@gmail.com.

2019 SALEEM SHAH AWARD: CALL FOR NOMINATIONS

The Saleem Shah Award is an annual award co-sponsored by the American Academy of Forensic Psychology (AAFP) and the American Psychology-Law Society (AP-LS), recognizing early career excellence and contributions to the field of psychology and law.  The focus of a nominee's contributions may be in any area of forensic psychology practice, research, or public policy. The recipient must have received the doctoral degree (or law degree, whichever comes later, if both have been earned) within the last 6 years (Year 2013 or later, inclusive of 2013). The award includes $2,000, as well as the opportunity to give the Saleem Shah Address at the next meeting of the American Psychology-Law Society.  Nominations must include (1) a letter detailing the nominee’s contributions to psychology and law and (2) a copy of the nominee’s vita.  Self-nominations will not be considered.  Nominations are due by midnight EST on November 30, 2019.

Nominations should be sent electronically, in PDF or Word format, to Bob Stinson, Psy.D., J.D., LICDC-CS, ABPP, President-Elect of AAFP: Stinson@StinsonAndAssociates.com.


DISSERTATION AWARDS

The American Psychology-Law Society confers Dissertation Awards for scientific research and scholarship relevant to the promotion of the interdisciplinary study of psychology and law. Students who complete dissertations involving basic or applied research in psychology and law in 2019 are encouraged to apply for these awards. First, second, and third-place awards will be conferred; winners will be invited to present their research at the annual AP-LS conference and receive a cash award.

Submissions are due by 12/31/19.

CALL FOR PAPERS

The ComplianceNet 2020 conference will take place on June 29–30, 2020, and will be hosted by the University College London (UCL) School of Public Policy/Department of Political Science and the UCL Global Governance Institute. We are currently accepting panel or paper submissions, with a submission deadline of January 24, 2020.  

If you are unfamiliar with the organization, ComplianceNet seeks to bring together scholars from a range of different disciplines to study the interaction between rules and human behaviour, broadly defined. The first two meetings have been highly successful, bringing together academics from business, criminology, economics, law, political science, psychology and sociology, among other fields. See the ComplianceNet website here for more details about its structure and goals. 

Prospective participants may submit either panel proposals (containing three to five thematically-linked papers) or individual paper proposals (which will be assigned to thematic panels after the submission deadline). The submission deadline is January 24, 2020.

LAW AND HUMAN BEHAVIOR UPDATE

Bradley D. McAuliff, JD, PhD (bradley.mcauliff@csun.edu)

California State University, Northridge

The Editorial Team recently received two important journal updates that we would like to share with Division 41.

This summer, Clarivate Analytics’ Social Science Citation Index released LHB’s impact factor for 2018. Measuring the average number of times articles from the journal published in 2016 and 2017 were cited in 2018, the impact factor decreased slightly from 2.867 in 2017 to 2.780 in 2018. The journal’s 5-year impact factor, measuring the average number of times articles from the journal published between 2013 and 2017 were cited in 2018, rose from to 3.245 to 3.372.

LHB’s ranking in the law category was 11 out of 148 and the journal was outranked by only one other psychology title, European Journal of Psychology Applied to Legal Context, which is an open access journal. In the social psychology category, LHB ranked 15 out of 63.

APA Journals also released its annual publishing report for LHB. The rate of submissions to the journal in 2018 increased 3% compared to 2017 and the rate is about 12% higher so far this year. The rejection rate decreased slightly from 85% last year to 81% in 2018. Editorial lag, or time between submission and first decision, also decreased from 6 weeks in 2017 to 4 weeks last year. Production lag, or time between acceptance and publication in an issue, was about 6 weeks for articles posted online and less than 3 months for articles included in issues.

The impact of LHB--combined with the steady submission rate, a short editorial and publication lag, and a rejection rate that hovers around 80%--suggests the journal remains very healthy. That said, we continue to need your best work! This is especially the case for clinical submissions, which are underrepresented and constitute roughly 20% of all submissions to the journal. Please consider submitting now and do not hesitate to contact me, or any of the Associate Editors (David DeMatteo, Amanda Zelechoski, Jennifer Hunt, Lora Levett, and Kyle Scherr) if you have any questions or we can help in any way.
